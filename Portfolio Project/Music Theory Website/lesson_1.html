Lesson 1
The Harmonic Series

All of nature is in a state of vibration - from the smallest subatomic particle to the greatest galaxy. Albert Einstein, himself, stated that “Everything in life is vibration.”

When a string is plucked, it not only vibrates along its entire length, but also along divisions of its length, as shown in Figure 1-1:

Figure 1-1
(Wikimedia Commons - public domain)

The tone produced from the string vibrating along its entire length is called the “fundamental” or “1st harmonic”. Each fractional division of its length (1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/5, 1/6, 1/7, etc.) produces a harmonic overtone.

Each of these overtones is a unique pitch, and the frequency of the pitch is inversely proportional to the string length.

For example, the 1st overtone of the fundamental tone is termed the “2nd harmonic”. The 2nd Harmonic vibrates at ½ the string length and its frequency is twice as much as that of the fundamental. The pitch of the 2nd harmonic is exactly one octave higher than the fundamental.

Likewise, the 3rd Harmonic vibrates at 1/3 the string length and its vibrational frequency is three times that of the fundamental. And so on…

The Harmonic Series can be considered as “Nature’s Chord” - these vibrational frequencies occur naturally when any medium is vibrated.

When you pluck a string on a guitar, or when a piano key is struck, all these overtones occur simultaneously. The fundamental tone sounds the loudest of all the harmonics, but if you listen very carefully, you may here the 2nd and 3rd harmonics.

Exercise 1:

If you have access to a grand piano, strike a key in the low register and you will not only hear the fundamental, but also its octave (2nd harmonic) and perhaps the 5th above that (3rd harmonic).

Exercise 2:

Pluck the low E string on an acoustic guitar or hollow-body electric guitar. Listen very carefully. You may hear its 1st overtone, especially when the volume of the fundamental is decreasing.

Unlike a solid-body electric guitar, an acoustic or hollow body electric has a resonating “box,” allowing the overtone frequencies of the vibrating string to be more easily heard.

Exercise 3:

Wherever you are, simply sit and listen to the sounds occurring in your environment. Pick out one sound in particular; for instance, the hum of the refrigerator. It doesn’t matter what the sound is - what matters is the conscious effort of listening. See if you can hear any overtones of the refrigerator hum or whatever sound you are listening to. Listen deeper still.

Then pick another sound in your environment and listen deeply to it, striving to hear its harmonics.

With practice, you will develop the ability to hear overtones wherever you may be, whether you are outside in nature, inside an office building, or at home.

As Einstein said, everything in life is vibration. Moreover, not only is all of life vibrating, but all vibrations in life are producing a symphony of harmonics.

Practice the Art of Listening and not only will you expand your aural perceptions, you will quell the constant chatter that occurs in the mind!
