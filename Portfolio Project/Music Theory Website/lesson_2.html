Lesson 2
The Chromatic Scale

The first musical overtone of any fundamental tone vibrates at twice the frequency and is half the string length of the fundamental.

With a tape measure, measure the distance from the nut to the bridge as shown in Figure 2-1.

Figure 2-1

You will notice that the 12th fret is exactly half the distance from the nut to the bridge. The note played on the 12th fret is the octave of the open string note and vibrates at twice the frequency.

If you pluck the open low E, which vibrates at 82.4 Hz (cycles per second), then its octave lies 12 frets above and vibrates at 164.8 cycles per second.

One octave above that E is the high open-string E, which vibrates at 329.6 Hz.

The vibrational frequencies for each open string of a guitar is as follows:

E: 329.6 Hz
B: 246.9 Hz
G: 196 Hz
D: 146.8 Hz
A: 110 Hz
E: 82.4 Hz

These are the fundamental tones for each string, and each of these tones has its own harmonic series.

The first overtone of the harmonic series is the octave; and, as you can see on the neck of your guitar, the octave contains twelve chromatic notes within it.

Exercise 1
The "C" Chromatic Scale

Play the C chromatic scale two octaves, starting on the 8th fret of the low E string. Music manuscript notation and tab for this are given below in Figure 2-2.

As you play, name/sing the note you are playing. After you have the fingerings memorized, simultaneously play the C chromatic scale while looking at the corresponding notes written on the music manuscript. In this way, you will quickly develop your ability to read music!

Exercise 2

Play the chromatic scale two octaves, ascending and descending, beginning on C, then on F, then Bb, and continue through the entire Circle of 5ths.

The music manuscript and tab for the Chromatic Scale Through the Circle of 5ths is available in the Music Theory for Jazz Blues Guitar ebook (coming soon!).

Exercise 3
Spider #1

The following exercise given in Figure 2-3 uses parts of the chromatic scale in a wonderful warm-up exercise. In all these examples, use the tab as a way to learn where the written notes are on the keyboard. With practice, you will soon be able to read music notation without the aid of tab.

Set your metronome to a comfortable tempo and play through the entire exercise. When you can play the exercise with no mistakes at a certain tempo, then increase the tempo. Strive for accuracy - listen to every note you play and make sure they sound consistent!

In this way, you will develop speed and accuracy in your playing. Enjoy!!
